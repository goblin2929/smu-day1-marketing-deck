1.2 · Customer & Market 05

What is a "Job to be Done"?

Customers don't really want your product. They "hire" it to make progress on something in their life. That progress is the job — and marketing's role is to connect what you sell to it.
Market the features and you compete on specs. Market the job and you understand why they choose you.

1.2 · Customer & Market 09

McDonald's asked: what job is a milkshake hired for?

Context
A long, boring morning drive. Alone in the car, with only one hand free.
The job
"Give me something filling that makes the commute less boring."
Struggles
Bagels are dry and crumbly. Bananas are gone in a minute. Coffee doesn't fill you up.
Desired outcome
Stay full and occupied the whole drive, with one hand and no mess.
So the milkshake wins: thick enough to last, drinkable one-handed, filling. Its real rivals are bananas and bagels, not other milkshakes.

1.3 · Positioning & Brand 12

Positioning is earned,
not announced.

Positioning · the input
The choice you make about how you want to be perceived relative to the alternatives.
Volvo didn't say "we're safe." In 1959 it invented the three-point seatbelt and gave the patent away free to every carmaker. It proved the position with a decision instead of claiming it.
Claims invite doubt. Proof lets people convince themselves.
1.3 · Positioning & Brand 13

You can't say your way out of what you are.

Brand · the output that compounds
The actual set of meanings, feelings, and associations that end up living in people's heads over time.
◆ The claim · GM, 1988
"This is not your father's Oldsmobile."
…but this was the car. Ads paired celebrities (Ringo Starr, William Shatner) with their kids to look young.
● The reality
Same boxy cars. Same showroom.
The tagline insulted the loyal older buyers who actually bought Oldsmobiles — and never won the young ones it chased. Nothing changed but the words.
The proof — in the numbers
1.2M cars at its 1984 peak → never above 500k after 1990 → brand dissolved in 2004, after 107 years.

1.7 · Measurement & Growth 28

Input. Output. Outcome.

Every marketing number is one of three things — what you put in, what the campaign produced, and what the business actually got. Most owners confuse the middle one for the last.
1 · Campaign Input
What you put in
  • •Ad spend / budget
  • •Content produced — posts, emails, ads
  • •Channels & time
→
2 · Campaign Output
How efficiently it ran
  • •Impressions & reach
  • •CTR · CPC
  • •CAC · ROAS
  • •LTV · LTV : CAC
→
3 · Business Outcome
What the business got
  • •# of new customers
  • •Active users
  • •Revenue & growth
Outputs feel good. Outcomes pay the bills. Never report one as if it were the other.
1.7 · Measurement & Growth 29

What you measure is what you chase.

Measurement steers strategy — chase the wrong number and you optimise toward the wrong business. There's even a law for it:
⚖ Goodhart's Law "When a measure becomes a target, it stops being a good measure."  Reward the team on leads → a CRM full of junk. Chase impressions → reach that never buys.
◆ Vanity metrics
Look impressive. Guide nothing.
  • •Impressions & reach
  • •Likes & followers
  • •Email open rate
  • •"We went viral"
● Actionable metrics
Tie to a decision.
  • •Conversion rate
  • •CAC & LTV : CAC
  • •Repeat purchase / retention
  • •Revenue per customer
If a number can't change a decision, it's decoration.
1.7 · Measurement & Growth 30

Spend $200 to win a customer
worth $800.

That is a healthy 4 to 1. Two things to check next: how long until you earn the $200 back, and which stage leaks the most.
You don't need perfect data. Know your CAC, your LTV, and your biggest leak.

2.1 · Social Media 02

Social media marketing is usually three workstreams.

Most owners think "social" is one thing — posting. It's really three different jobs, each reaching a different audience.
01 · Owned
Your own social accounts
  • The shop that's open 24 hours — an extension of your website
  • Most followers are your loyal community, or people just learning about you
  • Use it for product announcements and to engage your most frequent, loyal customers
02 · Paid Social
Ads you buy on the platforms
  • You buy ads on Instagram or Facebook
  • Mostly used for user acquisition — reaching new users and prospects
  • Puts you in front of people who don't follow you yet
03 · Influencers
Third-party creators
  • You engage influencers on the social platforms
  • They expose their own audience to your products and services
  • Borrowed reach and borrowed trust, beyond your own following

Collaborating with individuals who have a strong, engaged following on social media to promote a brand, product, or service.

  • Mega: 1M+ followers (broad reach)

Strategies

2.1 · Social Media 22
Strategic Approaches & Hybrid Models
📣 Campaigns
✓ What works
  • Creative storytelling, not forced placement
  • Content fits naturally in creator's style
  • Clear narrative (launch, feature, occasion)
  • Authentic usage that feels genuine
⚡ Strengths
  • Flexibility with wider range of creators
  • Ideal for specific launches or products
  • Quick testing of formats and angles
  • More control over cost and scope
⚠ Risk to watch
  • Feels transactional if over-scripted
  • Weak results if creator–brand fit is poor
"A campaign fails not because it's one-off — but because it feels like an ad."
★ Brand Ambassadors
✓ What works
  • Ongoing, natural integration
  • Repeated exposure builds familiarity
  • Strong creator–brand alignment
  • Less "selling", more lived experience
⚡ Strengths
  • Trust compounds over time
  • Content feels more organic and credible
  • Higher long-term ROI
  • Easier content reuse across channels
⇄ Trade-offs
  • Requires longer commitment
  • Smaller creator pool & less flexibility
"Ambassadors don't sell faster — they sell more convincingly."
⤬ Hybrid Strategy
Launch & awareness
  • Use macro influencers to kickstart visibility
  • Create cultural or conversational momentum
Sustained trust
  • Use mid-tier or nano as ambassadors
  • Better engagement & cost-efficiency
  • Builds stronger trust over time
The Golden Rule
Scale awareness fast. Build trust steadily.

2.1 · Social Media 37
Understanding Reach vs. Engagement vs. ROI
👁 Reach
Definition
Measures the total number of unique people who see your content. It reflects the extent of your content's visibility.
Primary Purpose
Drives Awareness
Key Metrics
  • Impressions
  • Video Views
  • Unique Visitors
  • Follower Growth
❤ Engagement
Definition
Measures how the audience interacts with content. It indicates how well content resonates.
High engagement signals interest and increases organic visibility via algorithm prioritization.
Primary Purpose
Signals Resonance
Key Metrics
  • Likes & Comments
  • Shares & Saves
  • Click-Through Rate (CTR)
📈 ROI
Definition
Measures the tangible business impact of social media efforts relative to the cost.
Primary Purpose
Validates Results
Key Metrics
  • Cost Per Acquisition (CPA)
  • Conversion Rate
  • Attributed Revenue
  • Customer Lifetime Value

2.2 · Email Marketing 43
Landing in the inbox, not the spam folder
◆ 1 · Prove it's really you
Authenticate your domain
  • SPF · DKIM · DMARC — DNS records that prove the email truly came from you
  • Set up once, in your email tool — then forget it
  • Since 2024, Gmail & Yahoo require them for bulk senders. No authentication → no inbox
● 2 · Keep a clean reputation
  • Keep spam complaints under 0.3% — the line Gmail & Yahoo draw
  • Remove inactive & bounced addresses — stop emailing people who never open
  • Use double opt-in · one-click unsubscribe (now required)
  • Warm up a new domain slowly; avoid image-only emails (a spam trigger)
A small, engaged list beats a big, cold one — every time. Reputation is earned one send at a time.
2.2 · Email Marketing 44

You don't hand-edit DNS or scrub lists by hand. A proper platform — Mailchimp, Klaviyo, HubSpot, Brevo — handles the plumbing.

Day 1 — Capstone · The answer

HYROX: position vs brand.

The clearest case all day of how the two differ — and why both are deliberate.
Positioning · the argument
Standardised fitness racing — the same format anywhere in the world (8×1 km runs + 8 functional stations) — for the everyday gym-goer who wants real competition. Unlike CrossFit, which is varied, elite and intimidating, or a marathon, which is pure running. The one sharp strategic choice is standardisation: because the format is identical everywhere, your time is comparable and you can qualify globally. It deliberately excludes the elite-only crowd and the purely non-competitive.
Brand · the reputation banked
The roar of the event hall, "I did a Hyrox" worn as a badge, the global community, the gyms that train people for it, the merch and the identity, the sense that it's the thing right now. What it banks is a feeling: belonging, accomplishment, being early to something.
Standardisation is the position — a deliberate, comparative choice. The tribe and the prestige are the brand — the reputation the market deposits at every event. Hyrox chose the format; the market chooses what it means to be a Hyrox person.
Day 1 — Capstone · The answer 59

HYROX: the offer, the channels, the loop.

The offer
One standardised race, identical worldwide, with no prerequisites — so it's attainable, measurable and shareable. The festival model does the rest: the race draws you in, and the event hall keeps you there for hours.
Capture demand · the channels
The heavy lifter is participant social: #Hyrox is around 390M TikTok views, and every athlete films their own race. Grassroots distribution through gym chains like F45 brings people in without ad spend. And the influencer model is inverted — the celebrities race, they don't pose.
The growth loop
They acquire through gym integration and social proof, convert with a single low-friction format, and keep people with rankings, the festival and belonging. Then 550,000 finishers each share a personal win, which brings the next ones in.
By 2025 the 700 athletes of 2017 had grown to 550,000 at $130M revenue, with most of the reach driven by participants themselves.

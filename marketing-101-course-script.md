# Marketing 101 for Business Owners — Lecturer's Course Script

**Audience:** business owners / founders / non-marketers (SMU exec-style).
**Goal:** by the end, an owner can say what they stand for, who it's for, how people find & choose them, and where their growth is leaking — and leave with a filled worksheet for their own business.
**Status:** script + example sourcing + infographic/image specs. Slides built afterward (decks already started: `positioning-vs-brand.html`, `marketing-101-fundamentals.html`).

---

## How to deliver this course (the teaching model)

**1. Active, not lecture-only.** Adults learn by doing. Every module follows **"I do → we do → you do"**: I explain the framework, we walk a real brand through it together, then *you* apply it to your own business. The "you do" is the point — they leave with worksheets, not notes.

**2. Concrete before abstract.** Open every module with a *hook* (a question or a real situation they recognise), then a *worked example*, and only then name the framework. People remember the milkshake, then the theory — not the reverse.

**3. One running spine.** The same picture recurs in every module: **create demand → capture it → convert → keep → measure.** By Module 6 they can place every tactic on it. The course ends by turning that spine into a *diagnostic* ("where's your leak?").

**4. A running case + their own business.** We thread **one familiar brand** as the worked example per module (varies by module: Red Bull, McDonald's, Volvo, IKEA, Dollar Shave Club, Amazon), and the **exercise is always "apply it to YOUR business."** Optional: have each table pick one business to carry through all six exercises.

**5. Module rhythm — 4 pages, ~25–30 min each:**
| Page | Beat | Time |
|------|------|------|
| 1 | **Hook + objective** — the problem this module solves | ~3 min |
| 2 | **Framework** — the named model, one infographic | ~7 min |
| 3 | **Worked example** — a real brand through the framework | ~7 min |
| 4 | **Exercise + debrief + takeaway** — apply to own business | ~10 min |

**6. Pacing for a half-day:** Module 0 (15 min) → Modules 1–6 (~25 min each ≈ 2.5 hrs incl. a break) → Capstone diagnostic (20 min). Trim exercises to fit a 90-min version; keep the hooks and examples.

**7. Slide discipline (for when we build):** minimal words, one example + one infographic per page, editorial style (matches the existing decks). Exercises get a worksheet slide + a timer.

# SECTION 1 — Marketing in the AI Era (AM)

*Badge-keyed to the live deck `smu-day1-full-deck.html` (dk1 slides 01–31). Every module runs the same beat — hook → framework → worked example → your-turn — on the running spine: **create demand → capture → convert → keep → measure.***

## Module 0 — What Marketing Actually Is

**Slide 01 — Hook: "In one word, what is marketing?"**
- **Ask the room, take answers, don't correct yet:** "In one word — what is marketing?" You'll get *ads, posting, branding, sales.* Put a few on the board. "Hold those — most are a *piece* of it, not the whole."
- The hook's only job: surface the narrow definition so the next slide can widen it.

**Slide 02 — Marketing = create demand, then capture it**
- **Say:** "Here's the whole job in one line: **create demand, then capture it** — getting the right people to choose you, again and again, at a profit. Most owners think marketing = ads or posting. It's bigger."
- **Walk the five questions on screen** — the spine of the entire day: **Who for · Why you · The offer · Demand (how they find you) · Proof (did it work).** "Every module today answers one of these."

**Slide 03 — Create demand vs capture demand (Red Bull / Google)**
- **Contrast the two sides:** "**Create demand** — Red Bull makes events and stunts you *seek out*; you weren't looking for an energy drink, they made you want one. **Capture demand** — Google catches you the second you type 'buy running shoes'; it can't *create* the want, only catch it."
- **The line that lands:** "Most small businesses only ever *capture* — then wonder why growth stalls. Nobody's creating the demand in the first place." *(Red Bull reel is clickable — play 10s if the room's cold.)*

**Slide 04 — Red Bull in the wild**
- **Say:** "Red Bull sells cans by *making media* — an F1 team, the Stratos space jump, the Air Race. The drink is almost the by-product."
- **Tie back:** "That's 'create demand' taken to the extreme. You don't need their budget — you need their *posture*: be worth seeking out."

## Module 1 — Customer & Market (Jobs to be Done)

**Slide 05 — What is a Job to be Done?**
- **Tie back, then open the idea:** "Module 0 left us with five questions, and the very first was *who for*. Most owners answer that with a demographic — 'women, 25 to 40, city, mid-income.' It feels like an answer, but it tells you nothing about why anyone actually buys. So we're going to answer *who for* in a sharper way. Here's the idea: **people don't really want your product — they hire it to make progress on something in their life, and that progress is the 'job.'** The classic line: nobody wants a quarter-inch drill, they want a quarter-inch hole — and really, they want the shelf on the wall and the small satisfaction of having handled it themselves."
- **The fork that decides everything:** "And this isn't just a nice phrase — it forks your whole strategy. If you market the *features* — the specs, the ingredients, the horsepower — you've trapped yourself competing on specs and price, because anyone can copy a feature list and undercut you. But if you market the *job* — the progress the customer is trying to make — you finally understand why they pick you over every alternative, and you can charge a premium for it. Hold onto that one word, *job*. The next four slides are nothing but tools for digging it out."

**Slide 06 — The JTBD framework (Miro)**
- **Say, pointing at the template:** "And you don't have to invent the method — this is the standard Jobs-to-be-Done canvas, the exact one professional product teams use to work out what their customers are really hiring them for. What it forces you to do is write the job down as one plain sentence in the *customer's* words, not yours. The link is live, grab it later — the reason I'm showing it now is just so you see this is a real, repeatable worksheet, not a vibe."
- **Hand off to the next slide:** "A blank canvas can be intimidating, though — so let me hand you the five questions that actually fill it in." *(Miro link clickable.)*

**Slide 07 — The Use Case Map (Reforge)**
- **Walk the five, slowly:** "These five questions turn a vague 'job' into a map you can actually act on. **The problem** they're trying to solve. **The persona** — who specifically has it. **The alternatives** — what they'd reach for instead of you. **The frequency** — how often the job even comes up. And **the why** — the deeper reason it matters to them. This framing is from Reforge."
- **The one they skip — and why it's expensive:** "Now watch the one every owner races past: *alternatives*. They'll lovingly describe their customer and the problem, then completely forget to ask what that person would do *instead* of buying from them. And that blank is almost always where the real competitor is hiding — and nine times out of ten it isn't another company, it's a spreadsheet, a DIY workaround, or simply doing nothing. Keep that idea warm — in two slides, a milkshake is going to prove it."

**Slide 08 — Functional / Emotional / Social**
- **Explain the three layers with the drill:** "Those questions surface the job — but here's what they won't tell you on their own: a job is almost never *only* practical. Take a cheap power drill. The **functional** job is the obvious one — a clean hole in the wall. But sitting underneath is an **emotional** job: feeling capable and in control instead of frustrated on a Saturday afternoon. And stacked on top is a **social** job: being seen — by your partner, your neighbour — as the kind of person who can just handle things. Same drill, three jobs layered on top of each other."
- **Why this is worth money, not just interesting:** "This matters because the moment you can see all three layers, you finally know everything you're *really* up against. The drill isn't only competing with other drills — it's competing with a YouTube tutorial, the handyman down the road, and the little voice that says 'eh, leave it for now.' Name the emotional and social jobs and you can speak to them directly in your marketing — which is something a spec sheet can never do for you."

**Slide 09 — The Milkshake (worked example)**
- **Tell the story, take your time:** "Now let me pour all of it — the job, the alternatives, the emotional layer — into one story you'll never forget. McDonald's spent years trying to sell more milkshakes the obvious way: new flavours, tweak the price, ask customers what they wanted. Nothing moved. Then a researcher asked a completely different question — not 'how do we improve the milkshake' but *'what job are people actually hiring this milkshake to do?'* So they watched, and a strange pattern jumped out: a huge share were bought early in the morning, by people on their own, taken to go. The job? Get through a long, boring, one-handed commute. And the milkshake turns out to be *perfect* for it — it's thick, so it lasts the whole drive; you can hold it in one hand on the wheel; and it's filling enough to kill the hunger till lunch."
- **Land the payoff:** "So now ask the magic question: what is that milkshake *actually* competing with? Not other milkshakes. **Its real rivals are a banana, a bagel, a doughnut** — the other things a bored commuter might grab on the way out the door. The instant you see the job, your whole competitive set changes — and so does how you'd improve the product: you'd make it *thicker*, not sweeter. That's this entire module, in one drink."

**Slide 10 — Your turn (discuss)** *(you do — ~9 min)*
- **Set it up, then step back:** "Your turn — and this is the part that actually changes your business, so please don't speed past the alternatives, because you just saw that's the classic mistake. The question on the table: **what job is your customer really hiring you to do?** Write the problem in *their* words, not your marketing words. Then list, honestly, what they'd do *instead* of you — a competitor, sure, but just as often a spreadsheet, a cheaper bodge, or simply doing nothing at all."
- **Timing and debrief:** "Give it **6 minutes on your own, then 3 with the person next to you.** I'll grab two examples after — ideally the ones where the *alternative* surprised you, because that's usually where the real insight is hiding."

## Module 2 — Positioning & Brand

**Slide 11 — Position ≠ Brand (Volvo)**
- **Define both, using the Volvo orbit on screen:** "**Position** = the one idea you own in a mind. **Brand** = everything that gathers around it. Volvo's position is **safety**; Swedish engineering, built-to-last, family-first all orbit that one word."
- **Spine tie:** "Position is this morning's *why you*, boiled down to a single word you can own."

**Slide 12 — Positioning is earned, not announced**
- **Say:** "Positioning is the *input* — the choice of how you want to be seen versus the alternatives. But Volvo didn't *say* 'we're safe.' In 1959 it invented the three-point seatbelt and **gave the patent away free** to every carmaker. It proved the position with a *decision*."
- **The line:** "Claims invite doubt. Proof lets people convince themselves."

**Slide 13 — Cautionary case: Oldsmobile** *(point at the car on the slide)*
- **Say:** "The flip side. Brand is the *output* that compounds — the meanings that end up in people's heads. In 1988 Oldsmobile ran **'This is not your father's Oldsmobile'** — celebrities and their kids, chasing young buyers. But it was the same boxy car, same showroom. **Nothing changed but the words** — so it insulted the loyal older buyers and never won the young ones."
- **The lesson:** "You can't *say* your way out of what you are."

**Slide 14 — How a brand gets built: three layers**
- **Walk the layers:** "**Decide** what you stand for (Nike: victory for every body; Lululemon: the 'sweatlife'; Love, Bonito: fashion for the Asian woman's body). **Express** it as a story with the customer as hero and you as the guide. **Prove** it through what you actually do. Each layer feeds the next."

**Slide 15 — What a brand marketer does: four moves**
- **Say:** "Building it was the last slide; this is what you *repeat* to grow it — Byron Sharp's *How Brands Grow.* **1 Own your position** (keep proving it). **2 Be impossible to forget** — same look, name, message for years. **3 Show up at the buying moment** — mental availability. **4 Be everywhere your buyers are** — physical/category availability."
- Keep this brisk — it's the bridge from building a brand into the daily job of growing one.

## Module 3 — The Offer

**Slide 16 — Your offer = product + price + packaging**
- **Say:** "The offer is everything the customer weighs *before* buying — the product, the price, and how it's bundled, guaranteed, delivered. Change any one and you change their decision."
- **Point at the canvas:** "It's the Value Proposition Canvas — match your **product / pain-relievers / gain-creators** to their **jobs / pains / gains.**"

**Slide 17 — Example: IKEA & Dollar Shave Club**
- **Say:** "**IKEA** — same chair, lower price; the *offer* is the strategy. **Dollar Shave Club** — $1/month, blades to your door; the offer built the brand before a cent on ads."
- **The diagnostic line:** "When marketing isn't working, the *offer* is often the real problem — not the ads."

## Module 4 — Demand & Channels

**Slide 18 — Match the message to the intent**
- **Say:** "Channels aren't good or bad — they match a *stage of intent.* **See** (widest, no intent: social, video, PR) → **Think** (considering: SEO/AEO, content, email) → **Do** (ready to buy: search ads, product pages) → **Care** (existing customers: email, loyalty, referral)."
- Keep this four-stage picture on the board all afternoon — it's the spine made practical.

**Slide 19 — Example: DSC spike vs HubSpot habit**
- **Contrast:** "**Dollar Shave Club** — one $4,500 launch video, 12,000 sign-ups in 48 hours: a *spike*. **HubSpot** — software bought once every few years, so they built a *content habit* to stay top-of-mind through the long wait. Find the stage you're missing, then build *one* channel that keeps paying off."

**Slide 20–23 — Campaign teardown: guess the objective** *(we do — run it as a quick game)*
- **Format:** "Four real campaigns. Before I reveal it — shout out the *objective*. No trick answers; each maps to a stage of the spine."
- **20 · Dollar Shave Club → Acquisition (a launch).** One $4,500 video, 12k sign-ups in 48h, built the whole brand before traditional ads.
- **21 · Novastacks (our own) → Discovery / new-customer awareness.** Search "best AEO agency Singapore" and Google's AI Overview names us first — customers discover us *inside the AI's answer*, not by scrolling ten blue links. "That's AEO — and it's exactly what this course also teaches you to do."
- **22 · Amazon 1-Click + Prime → Conversion (+ retention).** 1-click makes buying effortless; Prime makes leaving costly.
- **23 · Spotify Wrapped → Retention & Advocacy.** Each user's own data becomes a shareable story — free reach that pulls new users in every December.

**Slide 24 — Your turn (discuss)** *(you do — ~11 min)*
- **Set up:** "Where's the *gap* in how customers find you? Map your channels onto **See / Think / Do / Care** — most owners sit only at 'Do.' Pick **one** channel to build first." **8 min · 3 min to share.**

## Module 5 — Conversion & Retention

**Slide 25 — Convert, then keep**
- **Say:** "Demand's useless if it leaks. **Convert** — remove friction so an interested person actually buys. **Keep** — build the habit: trigger → action → reward, repeated. **Advocate** — happy customers bring the next ones in at low cost."

**Slide 26 — Example: Amazon & Spotify**
- **Say:** "**Amazon** — 1-click converts, Prime retains. **Spotify Wrapped** — retains *and* advocates in one move: a shareable yearly moment that keeps users and pulls new ones in. Removing friction and giving a reason to return usually beats a bigger ad budget."

**Slide 27 — Your turn (discuss)** *(you do — ~10 min)*
- **Set up:** "Where do you *lose* people, and why don't they come back? Walk your own buying process, name **one** point of friction to remove this week, and **one** reason to give them to return." **7 min · 3 min to share.**

## Module 6 — Measurement & Growth

**Slide 28 — Input. Output. Outcome.**
- **Say:** "Every marketing number is one of three things: **Input** (what you put in — budget, content, time), **Output** (how efficiently it ran — impressions, CTR, CPC, CAC, ROAS), **Outcome** (what the *business* got — customers, revenue, growth)."
- **The trap:** "Most owners confuse the middle for the last. Outputs *feel* good; outcomes pay rent."

**Slide 29 — What you measure is what you chase (Goodhart's Law)**
- **Say:** "Measurement steers strategy — chase the wrong number and you optimise toward the wrong business. **Goodhart's Law: when a measure becomes a target, it stops being a good measure.** Reward leads → a CRM full of junk. Chase impressions → reach that never buys."
- **Sort them live:** "**Vanity** (look impressive, guide nothing): impressions, likes, followers, open rate, 'we went viral.' **Actionable** (tie to a decision): conversion rate, CAC, LTV:CAC, repeat purchase."

**Slide 30 — Example: a healthy 4:1**
- **Say:** "Spend **$200** to win a customer worth **$800** — a healthy **4:1**. Then check two things: how long until you earn the $200 back, and which stage leaks most. You don't need perfect data — know your **CAC**, your **LTV**, and your **biggest leak.**"

**Slide 31 — Activity 2: Diagnose your leak** *(you do — ~12 min)*
- **Set up the diagnostic:** "Four places it leaks — **Demand** (no one finds you → fix channels), **Positioning** (they find you but don't get *why you* → fix the message), **Conversion** (interested but don't buy → fix the offer), **Retention** (buy once then leave → fix the experience)."
- **The rule that matters:** "Fix the **earliest serious leak first** — it's rarely the loudest one. This is the whole morning turned into a tool you'll keep using."

## Block 1 — Social Media Marketing

**Slide 02–03 — What social media is / the three workstreams**
- **Ask first:** "Who runs their own Instagram or Facebook for the business? What do you actually use it for?" (Most say *posting*.)
- **Say:** "Your account is a shop open 24 hours — an extension of your website. But 'social media marketing' is actually *three* jobs people lump together: **owned** (your account — talking to people who already follow you), **paid** (ads you buy to reach *new* prospects), and **influencers** (renting someone else's audience and trust). Different money, different jobs."
- **Spine tie:** owned = keep the loyal warm; paid + influencers = create new demand.
- ▸ *Interactive:* one-hand poll — "Mostly owned, paid, or influencer right now?" Most SMEs only do owned → that's the gap you'll name later.

**Slide 04 — Social Media Channels**
- **Say:** "You don't need to be on all of these — be where *your* buyer already is." Kill the FOMO.
- **Ask:** "Where does your customer actually spend time — Facebook groups? TikTok? Xiaohongshu?" One sentence each.

**Slide 05 — 15 Most Popular Social Networks**
- **Say (don't read the chart):** "The point isn't the ranking — it's that 'social media' is a dozen rooms with different crowds. Pick the room your customer is in, not the biggest room."

**Slide 06–08 — Channel guides: Facebook / Instagram / TikTok**
- Run fast, as a *matching* exercise. **Ask each:** "Whose business fits this one?" Facebook = older, service, community; Instagram = visual (food/beauty/fitness/fashion/travel); TikTok = reach + younger + entertainment.
- **Say:** "Same content does *not* work everywhere — that's the whole next block."

**Slide 09 — TikTok: from feed to checkout (commerce)**
- **Say:** "Here's what changed. TikTok isn't a video app any more — it's a *storefront*. Three things now live in the feed: **TikTok Shop** (tap a product in a video, buy in-app; creators earn commission), **Live Shopping** (hosts sell in real time — demos, Q&A, limited drops), and **Local Services**, still emerging — group-buy vouchers for restaurants and salons, redeemed in store (already huge on Douyin in China)."
- **The number that lands it:** "Southeast Asia TikTok Shop did **$45.6 billion** in 2025 — *ten times* what it did three years ago. Indonesia alone is $13.1B, its #2 market in the world. This isn't a Western thing — it's *our* region leading it." _(source: TNGlobal / DealStreetAsia, 2026)_

**Slide 10 — How brands win on TikTok**
- **Frame:** "You don't *buy* your way onto TikTok — you earn it. The algorithm rewards native, entertaining content, not polished ads. Two levers:"
- **1 · Ride the sound:** "Trending audio and original sounds. Jumping on a trend early gets ~376% more reach than your normal post. **e.l.f. Cosmetics** made an *original song* (#eyeslipsface) → 5M+ user videos, ~7 billion views, a real sales spike."
- **2 · Hire the right creators:** "Micro and nano creators with genuine trust out-convert celebrities — 8%+ engagement. Use affiliate links so every sale is trackable. **Dunkin'** went micro-influencer → 1B+ views; **Tarte** trend-jacked + went live → 600k units sold."
- **Land it:** "Raw and real beats polished. The algorithm gives an unknown brand a fair shot — *if the content earns it.*" _(sources: AnyRoad case studies; Influee; StackInfluence)_

**Slide 11 — Resources & tooling for influencer intelligence on TikTok**
- **Say:** "Don't pay until you need to. Three jobs, three toolkits. **(1) Find creators &amp; ideas → TikTok Creative Center — free.** Trending organic videos, sounds, hashtags &amp; creators by region — filter it to Singapore. It's effectively 'an ad library, but for organic.' **(2) Manage &amp; vet influencers → organic analytics** (Exolyt, Trendpop, Analisa.io, EnsembleData) — check real engagement and spot fake followers *before* you pay a creator. **(3) Sell on TikTok Shop → e-commerce intel** (Kalodata, FastMoss, Pipiads, Shoplus) — what's selling, which creators drive the most GMV, competitor intel."
- **Rule of thumb:** "Free Creative Center for inspiration → an analytics tool the moment you hand a creator money → a Shop spy tool the moment you open a TikTok Shop. **Most owners never need beyond free.**" _(source: TikTok Creative Center — ads.tiktok.com/business/creativecenter)_

**Slide 12 — Instagram vs TikTok**
- **The one-liner to teach:** "**Instagram is your shop window. TikTok is the street performer that pulls in the crowd.**"
- **Say:** "Instagram — polished, aspirational; people *follow* brands they already know; the aesthetic grid builds a curated image. TikTok — raw, entertaining; the *algorithm* shows you to strangers who don't follow you; trends and sounds make unknowns blow up. A café's job on Instagram is a beautiful flat-lay; on TikTok it's a messy 'how it's made' clip that goes viral and puts a queue out the door."
- ▸ *Interactive:* "Which one are you treating like the other? Most owners post Instagram-perfect content on TikTok and wonder why it flops."

**Slide 13 — Xiaohongshu (RedNote)**
- **The differentiator (lead with this):** "Don't think of it as another video feed. Xiaohongshu — RedNote — is a *search engine for buying decisions*. Before a facial, a stroller, a trip, a restaurant, people **search it and read real users' 'notes'** to decide. And the killer bit: **every post is an SEO asset** — a good note keeps pulling traffic for *months*, while IG/TikTok content dies in days. One line for the room: **Instagram = pretty · TikTok = fun · Xiaohongshu = useful & searchable.**"
- **Who's on it — it's a *language*, not a nationality:** "~78% women, 18–35, mid-to-upper income. The audience is defined by **Mandarin language + Chinese consumer culture, not the passport** — a Singaporean-Chinese, a Hongkonger and a mainlander are the *same* XHS crowd. Strongest for beauty, fashion, travel, F&B, wellness. And practically: **Chinese tourists plan their Singapore trip on it.** In SEA, Malaysia is its #2 market outside China; Malaysia + Singapore ≈ 4.3M users."
- **How brands win — KOC seeding (the specific tactic):** "Not one big KOL. You seed **many small 'Key Opinion Consumers'** — real users posting authentic notes (种草, 'planting the desire') — plus keyword-searchable content, so you surface when someone looks you up. Volume of genuine reviews > one polished ad."
- **Land it:** "In Singapore, SMEs and luxury labels are already shifting budget here in 2026. If you sell beauty, food, travel or wellness to affluent, Chinese-speaking women — this is your channel, in their words, not your ad." _(sources: Fortune; Hashmeta; Halo Tech / WE Interactive SG; Comms8, 2025–26)_

**Slide 14 — Xiaohongshu: see it live (demo)** *(do this in the app, not the browser)*
- **Set it up:** "Let me show you the research actually happening. I'll search the way a customer would." Open the **Xiaohongshu app** (web makes you log in — don't fight it live) and search a real query: **新加坡美食** ("Singapore food"), **新加坡好去处** ("places to go in SG"), or **新加坡 facial** (a buying decision).
- **Narrate what they see:** "Look — a grid of *notes*, not ads. This is people researching. Now open a good one and notice why it works: a **keyword-rich title** (so search finds it), a **strong cover image** (the thumbnail earns the tap), an **honest, detailed guide** (price, must-order, best time to avoid the crowd), and **lots of saves** — people bookmark it for later."
- **Make it concrete:** "These aren't theoretical — XHS sent waves of tourists to Singapore spots like **Fort Canning's tree tunnel**, the **Old Hill Street Police Station**, and **Jumbo's chilli crab.** A single good note moved real foot traffic."
- **Tie back:** "So a 'good post' here isn't a pretty photo — it's a *useful, searchable guide*. Write the thing your customer is already searching for." _(source: SmartLocal / Malay Mail on XHS-driven SG tourism, 2024–25)_

**Slide 15 — Types of posts that engage**
- **Say:** "Four formats that earn attention: How-to (teach), Behind-the-scenes (show the humans), Sales/Announcements (the ask), Memes/Entertaining (be likeable). Most SMEs only post the *ask* — then wonder why no one engages."
- ▸ *Interactive:* "Which of the four do you *never* post? That's probably your quickest win."

**Slide 16 — Are you ready for social media? (Readiness checklist)**
- **Say:** "Five honest checks before you commit — purpose, audience, capability, governance, measurement. The killer is *capability*: can you post consistently for a year? If not, do *one* channel well, not three badly."
- Frame as a gut-check, not a quiz — read the five headers, let it sit.

**Slide 17 — What success looks like on social**
- **Say:** "Social mostly does awareness, trust, consideration, retention. Notice what's at the bottom: **conversion is supportive, not primary.** Don't judge a brand post by direct sales — that's email's job. Judge it by whether the right people remember you."
- **Spine tie:** this is *See / Think*, not *Do*.

## Block 2 — Influencers & UGC

**Slide 18–19 — Influencer / AI influencer / content creators**
- **Hook:** "Marketing's oldest trick: if people don't trust *you* yet, stand next to someone they already trust." That's influencers in one line.
- On 18: "And now some of those 'people' aren't people — virtual/AI creators with hundreds of thousands of followers. The judgement is identical: does *their* audience trust them for *this* decision?"
- **Types, fast (folded in here):** "Mega → Macro → Micro → Nano. Counter-intuition: *smaller is often better.* A nano creator with 8k followers who genuinely loves you out-converts a celebrity, because the trust is real."

**Slide 20 — Why are you using influencers? (Bad → Better → Best)** ← *key slide of the block*
- **Ask first:** "If you hired an influencer tomorrow, how would you pick them?" Most say *biggest following* → that's the **bad** answer.
- **Walk the ladder:** Bad = big following. Better = in our category. **Best = whose audience already trusts them for this specific decision.** "Different objective, different creator."

**Slide 21 — Not all influencers are equal (vetting)**

- **Say:** "Four dials to turn per platform: long vs short, static vs video vs carousel, search-driven vs feed-driven, professional vs casual. You're not making new content — you're *re-cutting* one piece."

**Slide 33 — Website & blog**
- **Say:** "Your website is the one room *you* own, where people come to be convinced — structure it for SEO and trust (testimonials, case studies). Blog earns the search traffic." (Callback to Section 1 *Do* stage.)

- **Say:** "Almost everyone meets your content on a phone, one thumb, half-distracted. Short paragraphs, big buttons, clear hierarchy. If it's hard to read on mobile, it doesn't exist."

**Slide 35 — Repurposing content across platforms** ← *money slide of the block*
- **Say:** "Write *one* strong piece — a blog post — then spin it into a LinkedIn article, an IG carousel, a TikTok, an email tip, a website FAQ. One idea, seven assets. AI does the re-cutting in seconds." Work smart, not more.

## Block 4 — Email Marketing

**Slide 39 — Why email still works for SMEs**
- **Hook:** "Email feels old. But it's the only channel where you **own the audience** — no algorithm decides who sees you. Highest ROI in marketing, and it's *yours*." This is the *Care / Keep* stage of the spine.

**Slide 40 — Crafting effective emails (subject → CTA)**
- **Say:** "Anatomy of an email that works: subject line (gets it opened — the whole game), preview text, header, one clear message, one clear CTA. **One email, one job.** Don't ask them to do five things."

**Slide 41 — Examples**
- **Walk briefly:** "Notice — one message, one button, scannable. Not a wall of text." Point at what's good.

**Slide 42 — Why? (life stages)**
- **Say:** "Email's superpower: you can meet someone at the *right moment*. A new subscriber and a 2-year customer should not get the same email. Relevance is the entire game."

**Slide 43 — Deliverability & Sender Reputation** *(why you land in spam vs inbox)*
- **Frame it as two questions:** "Whether your email reaches the inbox comes down to two things — *can the inbox prove it's really you*, and *do people actually want your mail*."
- **1 · Prove it's you (authentication):** "SPF, DKIM, DMARC — three DNS records that prove the email came from your domain. You set them up *once* in your email tool. This isn't optional any more: **since 2024 Gmail and Yahoo require them** for anyone sending in volume. No authentication, no inbox."
- **2 · Keep a clean reputation:** "Keep spam complaints **under 0.3%** — that's the literal line Gmail and Yahoo draw. Remove people who never open. Use double opt-in and a one-click unsubscribe (now required). Warm a new domain up slowly. Don't send image-only emails — classic spam trigger."
- **The one line that matters:** "A small, engaged list beats a big, cold one *every time*. Every send either builds or burns your reputation."
- ▸ *Honest aside for the room:* "You don't need to memorise SPF vs DKIM — next slide shows how much of this your tool just does for you."

**Slide 44 — Let the tool do it** *(reassurance)*
- **Say:** "Good news: you don't hand-edit DNS records or scrub lists by hand. A real email platform — Mailchimp, Klaviyo, HubSpot, Brevo — does the plumbing: **auto-removes bounces/unsubscribes/complaints, walks you through SPF/DKIM/DMARC, runs the sending servers and their IP reputation, segments out your dead weight, and shows a deliverability dashboard** (HubSpot and Klaviyo especially)."
- **Land the takeaway:** "So your job is small and human: a **clean list**, **relevant content**, and **don't blast**. The tool handles the rest. The biggest mistake isn't a technical one — it's emailing people who stopped caring."

**Slide 45 — Email in the wild: Example 1 — nurture / value (Shopify Partners)**
- **Walk the screenshot:** "A relationship email, not a sell. Three things: the **subject is personalised** — 'Tina, attract more clients…' — it earns the open. It leads with *your* goal, not their product. And there's **one job, one CTA** — a single 'Learn more', no clutter. Plus a verified sender and easy unsubscribe — the deliverability hygiene from the last slide, in practice."

**Slide 46 — Email in the wild: Example 2 — triggered automation (HubSpot)**
- **Walk the screenshot:** "This one nobody pressed send on. It fired off a *behaviour* — 'inactive for 90+ days' — not a calendar. It names the user, the date, and the *exact* action to stop it, with the action in bold. This is the automation we're about to cover, sitting in a real inbox."
- **Bridge:** "Hold this image — the next few slides are how you build exactly this."

**Slide 47 — Email in the wild: Example 3 — promotional / launch (Dickie Bush)**
- **Walk the screenshot:** "A promo email sells the *click*, not the product. The subject is curiosity plus a hard number — '[Free Masterclass] The email flows behind $700k/month.' It reads like a note from a person — 'Hey there, Dickie here' — and it borrows trust with named speakers. Every line drives one CTA: save your seat."

**Slide 48 — Email is not a broadcast channel** *(mindset)*
- **Say:** "The #1 SME email mistake: blasting the *same* email to everyone, like a megaphone. Email is a *conversation*, not a broadcast. Best for activation, retention, churn-prevention — not shouting."

**Slide 49–51 — Automation: what it is / why / the core flows**
- **Say:** "Automation = the right email fires on *behaviour*, not on a calendar. Someone signs up → welcome. Goes quiet → win-back. Runs while you sleep. **Manual emails react late; automated emails act early.**"
- On 51: "You don't need dozens of flows — just the right few: welcome, activation, engagement nudge, re-engagement, milestone."
- ▸ *Interactive:* "Do you have even a *welcome* email today? That's the first one to build."

**Slide 52–54 — Lifecycle marketing / Supermom example / content by stage** *(we do)*
- **Say:** "Lifecycle = different message for where the customer *is*. Supermom nails it: pregnant mom, newborn, toddler, back-to-work — same brand, completely different needs. One email to all of them feels irrelevant, even insensitive."
- On 54: "What changes by stage: tone, frequency, depth, the CTA. **Relevance beats frequency** — fewer, righter emails win."

**Slide 55 — Bringing automation + lifecycle together**
- **Say:** "Put the two together: **Behaviour × Lifecycle = the right email.** What they *did* + where they *are* → what you send. That's smart email."

**Slide 56 — Common mistakes SMEs make**
- **Say:** "Recap of what *not* to do: blasting everyone, over-emailing, ignoring lifecycle, measuring opens instead of behaviour. **More emails ≠ better results.**"

# DAY 1 CAPSTONE — Case Study: HYROX *(end-of-day exercise, badge 58)*

**Format:** questions on screen first. Groups discuss ~10 min, then you map their answers on the board against the day's spine (**create demand → capture → convert → keep → advocate**). The point: HYROX quietly does *the entire day* — it's the whole framework in one brand.

**Set it up:** "Last thing today. A fitness race nobody had heard of in 2017 is now a $130M brand with half a million athletes. You now have every tool to explain *why*. Don't guess — use the framework."

**Let them struggle first, then reveal — answers mapped to the day:**

**1 · Create demand — positioning / brand / JTBD**
- **Positioning:** *"Fitness for every body."* A standardised, attainable race — the opposite of CrossFit (technical barrier) or a marathon (months of solo training). Their insight: *the highest barrier in fitness is psychological, not physical.* They removed it.
- **Brand:** community, measurable progress, and *"feel like a pro athlete."* Same format worldwide = **personal, measurable, shareable** — that's the brand promise, baked into the product.
- **Job to be done:** "Give me a real, attainable goal to train for, a community to belong to, and let me feel like an athlete." Fitness as a *social arena* (78% of younger APAC consumers train for community; 58% make friends through it). Its real rivals: marathons, CrossFit — and *doing nothing.*

**2 · Capture demand — channels (the clever part)**
- **Grassroots / owned:** embedded its training method into existing gym chains (e.g. F45) — distribution without heavy ad spend.
- **Participant-generated social (the engine):** #Hyrox ≈ **390M TikTok views, 469k+ posts — mostly shot by athletes themselves.** Each participant is their own media channel (training → race day → recap).
- **Inverted influencer model:** celebrities (SHINee's Minho, *Physical: 100* stars) **race, they don't pose.** Vulnerable pre-race content, 200k+ views at minimal cost. Authentic > endorsement.
- **Authenticity-first partnerships:** Puma (co-branded Deviate NITRO shoe), Red Bull (challenges), AirAsia, Lululemon, AIA — partners must *belong* to the community.

**3 · The growth loop — acquire / convert / keep / advocate**
- **Acquire:** gym integration + social proof.
- **Convert:** low-friction entry — no prerequisites, one standardised format, shared gym training culture.
- **Keep:** the festival model — the race draws you, "Hyrox House makes you stay six hours"; measurable rankings; community belonging.
- **Advocate:** **550,000 participants = 550,000 content creators.** Every finisher shares a *personal victory* → pulls in the next cohort. A self-amplifying flywheel.

**The one-line payoff (write it on the board):** *"Remove the psychological barrier + make the format standardised and shareable → every participant becomes a marketing channel."* That's not luck — it's the whole day's spine, executed.

**Tie back to their own business:** "You don't have 550k athletes. But the move is copyable — what's the *one psychological barrier* stopping people from choosing you, and how could you make your customers want to *share* the win?"
